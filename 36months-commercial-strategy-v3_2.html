The opportunity

The void is the opportunity.

The campaign successfully raised the social media access age from 13 to 16. But legislation and regulation can only go so far — and if the void is not filled, the gravitational pull of low effort dopamine hits will ultimately win. The unintended consequence is stark: a core identity, social, and dopamine system has been removed from teens without a replacement. That gap is not a problem — it is a category-defining commercial opportunity.

"No one has yet built the operating system for adolescence without social media. 36 Months has the brand, the trust, and the mandate to build it first."

The long game

This isn't kicking the ball down the road.

The most important distinction in this strategy is the difference between two cohorts — and why this platform must be built to serve both.

Cohort 1 — Today's teens (13–16)
Filling the void
These kids had social media — and now it's gone. For them, the platform must replace what was taken: status, identity, entertainment, and social connection. The experience must be compelling enough to fill that gap genuinely, not just as a substitute.
Cohort 2 — Tomorrow's teens
Never knowing the alternative
These kids will grow up with this platform as the default. They won't experience the loss of social media — they'll inherit a world where real life already offers more status, more fun, and more belonging. The question of "going back" won't arise in the same way.

"When real life is genuinely more rewarding than the feed — in ways that are visible, social, and fun — the gravitational pull back to social media weakens with each generation. This is how you build a lasting behaviour shift, not a temporary one."

The long-term commercial proposition depends on this. A platform that only serves today's cohort is a programme. A platform that shapes how the next generation comes of age is infrastructure — and infrastructure compounds in value over time.

Now — 2025
The legislation lands
Social media access removed for under-16s. A void exists. Today's 13–16 cohort needs replacement structures urgently. The platform launches as the answer.
2026–2028
The platform becomes the norm
Schools adopt the measurement layer. Corporates build jobs and skills programs. Real-world status systems become culturally visible. For the incoming cohort, this is simply how things are.
2029 onwards
The gravitational shift
Kids turning 16 and gaining social media access find that it competes with — rather than dominates — a rich, rewarding real world. The platform has done its job not by restricting, but by building something better. 36 Months becomes a category, not a campaign.

Platform model

Three layers. One system.

A stacked platform that builds on itself — data enables programs, programs enable a marketplace, the marketplace funds the data layer.

Layer 1 Measurement — Identity & Wellbeing Index Foundation

The NAPLAN for wellbeing. Tracks self-esteem, belonging, resilience, and agency — delivered via school dashboards and parent tools. Establishes the category standard and becomes the data backbone for everything above it. Critically, it provides the longitudinal evidence that the platform works — across both cohorts, across generations.

SaaS to schools Parent dashboard ESG reporting Benchmarking Longitudinal data
Layer 2 Experience — Programs & Community Intervention

Replaces dopamine loops with earned progress and real feedback. Jobs for kids, offline-first challenges, crew-based identity, and inter-school competitions. Designed to be genuinely fun and status-generating — so that when kids hit 16, real life already feels more rewarding than the feed. The cultural quality of every experience is backed by Finch's world-class production and events network.

Big ideas

Make real life more rewarding than the feed.

Teenagers don't adopt programs — they adopt things that give them status in front of other teenagers. These ideas are designed to be social without social media, highly visible, and culturally contagious. The test for every idea: would a 15-year-old actually want this?

"You're not building a program for teenagers. You're building a culture teenagers want to be part of."

F
Creative partner
The cultural credibility of these ideas depends entirely on execution. Finch — a globally renowned production company — brings world-class capability in film, live events, and youth culture to make this platform feel like something teenagers actually want to be part of.
01 — Status systems
The Reputation Graph
Earned, visible status based on skills, creativity, and contribution. Titles like Builder, Connector, Leader — culturally designed, not corporate. Portable across schools and communities.
02 — Physical signals
Limited-edition wearables
Streetwear-inspired physical status markers designed by top creatives — and in time, co-created with status-rich teenagers themselves. If kids want to wear it, the platform has won. Think festival wristbands meets sneaker culture. Finch's network of designers, directors and cultural makers is the engine behind the aesthetic.
03 — Real-world virality
National challenge system
Constantly evolving missions and team competitions — designed like TikTok trends but executed in the real world. Seasonal drops create FOMO and cultural rhythm.
04 — Belonging
Crew-based identity
Small named crews (5–10) compete and build together. Follower count replaced by crew reputation. Inter-school rivalry extended beyond sport — State of Origin for identity.
05 — Public stages
36 Months Festival
Think SXSW meets X Games meets Coachella — but for teenagers. High-production events in public spaces and brand environments. Only accessible through participation. Feels like culture, not education. Just being there has status. This is where Finch's live event and experience capability turns the concept into something genuinely iconic.
06 — New cool
The "No Phone" badge
A visible symbol of opting out that becomes a status flex. Works only if culturally desirable — this is where the creative and narrative firepower of the Finch network is critical.

Risks & mitigation

Five risks to manage.

Commercialising a cause Ringfence the mission. Transparent impact reporting. Measurement layer makes outcomes undeniable, not performative.
Low teen engagement Co-design with teens from day one. Prioritise intrinsic rewards. Apply the same creative firepower used in the campaign.
School adoption friction Align to existing curriculum and wellbeing mandates. Reduce admin load — make it easy to say yes.
Only serving today's cohort Build for the next generation from the start. Longitudinal design, not programme logic. Platform must compound in value over time.
Credibility scrutiny Media scrutiny is already emerging. Independent measurement and published outcomes are the shield — not PR.
